It's On You

Reflection, Knowledge, and Trust in a New Media Environment

Senior Honors Thesis · University of Wisconsin-Madison · May 2023
Advisor: Dr. Katherine Cramer

"Wherever the people are well-informed, they can be trusted with their own government." — Thomas Jefferson

For a democracy to function properly, trust must exist within the polity. Trust in the government working for the people. Trust in the government's processes. And trust in one's fellow citizens. Without the presence of trust, effective governing and a healthy society is hard to achieve.

This thesis analyzes how a rapidly changing media landscape—marked by fragmentation, algorithmic curation, and pervasive social media—has contributed to declining trust in American political institutions. Drawing on Gallup data over 50 years, the research shows confidence has dropped in 19 of 20 major institutions, with polarization deepening the divide.

The paper traces the evolution from a text-heavy society to one dominated by television and social media, examining how Facebook's News Feed revolutionized—and complicated—news consumption. Human psychology's desire for instant gratification, combined with algorithmic power, has created a highly saturated landscape where fake news spreads easily and reflection becomes difficult.

To reverse these trends, I argue that individuals must cultivate what Aristotle called phronesis—practical wisdom. In today's media environment, this means consuming news more responsibly, questioning sources, seeking diverse perspectives, and dedicating time to genuine reflection rather than reactive scrolling.

The burden falls on everyone: on social media platforms to prioritize transparency and healthy discourse over engagement metrics, on journalists to produce robust and accurate reporting, and on each of us to foster the intellectual curiosity necessary to see past our curated feeds.

Systemic change starts with individual action. If we, as a collective society, can achieve this recalibration, our political institutions can begin to regain the trust they've lost—and we can rebuild the shared understanding that democracy requires.
